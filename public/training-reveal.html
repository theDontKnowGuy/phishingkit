SECURITY TRAINING SIMULATION

Oy Oy Oy. You just entered your company's account password into a fake website.

The previous pages were NOT real Google authentication pages, and this was NOT a real company gift selection portal.
This was a controlled security training exercise designed to help you recognize and avoid phishing attacks.

If this had been a real phishing attack, your login credentials would now be compromised. The attackers could have:

  • Gained access to your Google account
  • Accessed your email, documents, and personal information
  • Used your credentials to access other company systems
  • Stolen sensitive company or personal data
  • Installed malware on your device

What made this look legitimate?

  • Professional appearance: The pages looked like real company and Google interfaces
  • Familiar branding: Used Google's actual colors, fonts, and logo
  • Personalization: Your username was pre-filled in the form
  • Urgency: Created a sense of urgency with gift selection deadline
  • Authority: Appeared to come from your company's holiday program

üõ°Ô∏è How to Protect Yourself from Phishing

  • Always verify the URL: Real Google sign-in pages always use accounts.google.com
  • Look for HTTPS: Legitimate sites use secure connections (https://)
  • Be suspicious of urgent requests: Phishers create false urgency
  • Never enter passwords from email links: Go directly to the website instead
  • Use two-factor authentication: Even if passwords are stolen, 2FA provides protection
  • Check email sender carefully: Look for misspellings in domain names
  • When in doubt, ask IT: Contact the IT security team if something seems suspicious
  • Use a password manager: It won't auto-fill on fake sites

Red Flags You Should Have Noticed

  • The URL was not accounts.google.com
  • The email field was pre-filled and disabled (Google doesn't do this)
  • The page accepted any password without validation
  • No two-factor authentication prompt
  • The "gift selection" premise may have seemed too good to be true

What to do if you suspect a real phishing attack:

  • Don't click any links in suspicious emails
  • Report it immediately to the IT security team
  • Change your passwords if you've already entered them
  • Run a security scan on your device
  • Monitor your accounts for suspicious activity

Remember: When it comes to cybersecurity, being cautious is better than being sorry!
